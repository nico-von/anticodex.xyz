Experiments showed, that when players(in a [[A prisoner's dilemma occurs when opposing players, acting rationally out of self-interest and fear of betrayal, choose to act against each other, even though both parties would be better off if they cooperate|prisoner's dillema]]) are allowed to punish cheating players but suffer a personal cost in exchange, a significant amount of punishments are still executed despite the cost([[People engage in the act of punishing 'social cheaters' despite it being a dominated strategy(as it doesn't give them any personal gains, and costs them something in exchange) because it provides them a psychological benefit or pleasure.]]) 

This [[The anticipation of punishment(to cheaters of implicit social contracts) greatly enhances cooperation in prisoner's dillema|greatly enhances cooperation in the prisoner's dillema]]
