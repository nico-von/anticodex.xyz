Italian words where the ending changes depending on the gender of what it describes 

1. Nouns
	1. [[The Italian for 'friend' is 'amico' for male, and 'amica' for female]]
	2. [['bambino' or 'bambina' is 'child' in Italian]]
	3. [['figlio' or 'figlia' is 'son' or 'daughter' in Italian]]
2. Possessives ([[Unlike English, where 'his' is used if the possessor is 'male' and 'her' if 'female', in Italian, the gender of the possessive word depends on the gender of the thing possessed, not of the possessor|changes according to the gender of the thing possessed]])
	1. [['tua' is the Italian feminine word for 'your']]
	2. [['mia' or 'mio' is the possessive 'my' in Italian]]
	3. [['Suo' or 'Sua' is the possessive 'his' or 'her' in Italian]]
3. Adjectives([[In Italian, adjective endings usually change according to gender, but not always, as some adjectives end in '-e' and do not vary between masculine and feminine]]). Examples:
	1. [['Pronto' is 'ready' in Italian]]
	2. [['Invitato' is masculine and 'Invitita' is feminine for 'invited' in Italian]]
	3. [['Interessato' or 'interessata' is 'interested' in Italian]]
	4. [['Sincero' and 'Sincera' is 'sincere' in Italian]]
	5. [['Tutto' is 'everything' in Italian]], [['Tutta' is the feminine form of 'Tutto']]

When the ending of a verb's past participle changes when used as a past tense:
1. [[In Italian, the ending of past participles used with 'To be' are changed according to the gender(or number) of the doer]]
2. [[In Italian, when a direct object pronoun precedes a past tense formed with 'avere', the verb's past participle changes its ending to match the gender of the pronoun]]
